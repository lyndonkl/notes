## Big Question

How do we ensure our beliefs actually connect to reality, and what does it mean to think rationally?

## Core Articles

1. **Making Beliefs Pay Rent (in Anticipated Experiences)** — A belief is only a genuine belief about reality if it constrains what you anticipate experiencing. A "belief" that makes identical predictions whether true or false is just verbal decoration.

2. **What Do We Mean By 'Rationality'?** — Rationality has two distinct modes: epistemic rationality (building an accurate map of reality) and instrumental rationality (achieving your goals). These are separable failure modes.

3. **Planning Fallacy** — We systematically underestimate timelines for projects by relying on detailed inside-view models. Reference class forecasting—using base rates from similar projects—outperforms our specific, granular planning.

## Core Argument

A belief must constrain your anticipated experiences, or it's not a belief about reality—it's just words. Rationality has two distinct dimensions: epistemic (accurate map) and instrumental (effective action), and these can fail independently. Our detailed internal models seduce us into false confidence; adding more granularity to a plan makes it *feel* rigorous but actually increases error through conjunction fallacy. Reference class forecasting—asking "how long did similar projects take?"—corrects for this systematic bias by ignoring the seductive specificity of our inside view.

## My Assessment

Kushal surfaced a crucial insight: detail in a plan increases its *perceived credibility* even as actual accuracy decreases. The inside view feels more trustworthy precisely because it contains specific, concrete details—but that friction, that sense of incompleteness in the outside view, is actually its epistemic strength. All three essays point to the same gap: the distance between our mental map (what we believe, how we plan) and the territory (what actually happens). Closing that gap requires operational criteria, not intuitive plausibility, and sometimes requires abandoning the appealing specificity of inside-view reasoning in favor of base-rate discipline.
