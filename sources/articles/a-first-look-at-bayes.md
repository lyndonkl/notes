## The Big Question
How can people use Bayesian reasoning practically in everyday thinking, rather than treating it as abstract mathematics?

## Core Argument
The series builds from concrete visualization (frequency diagrams) through formal frameworks (odds form, proportional form) to applied epistemological principles (extraordinary vs ordinary evidence). The central argument is that reasoning in relative odds — prior odds × likelihood ratio = posterior odds — provides a more intuitive and less bias-prone way to update beliefs than relying on raw intuition or thinking in pure probabilities. The proportional form simplifies this further by normalizing one side of the odds to 1, reducing the cognitive work to tracking a single number.

## My Assessment
The math is not new — I encountered Bayes' theorem in undergraduate studies. What's valuable here is the cognitive reframing: thinking in relative odds rather than probabilities, and using log odds as mental shorthand. These are interfaces to the math that make it usable in my head, which academic teaching never provided. The series is pedagogically strong — it builds one concept per step without rushing. The main gap is that every example uses clean, given numbers, and real-world Bayesian reasoning requires estimating your own priors and likelihoods. I'm experiencing this gap directly in my cricket forecasting work, where I convert game phases into bullish/bearish/neutral scenarios and use heuristics to turn those into point adjustments on a base rate from head-to-head records.
