## The Big Question
What does it actually mean for something to count as "evidence" about a claim, and what rules must any rational process of belief-updating obey?

## Core Argument
Yudkowsky builds a three-step argument that reframes evidence in purely causal-probabilistic terms. First: an observation is evidence about a target only if its probability would have been different depending on the state of the target — evidence is a correlated event produced by a causal chain. Second: this forces a symmetry — if observing E would shift your belief toward H, then the prior expectation of E being observed at all cannot, on average, shift your belief at all, because the failure to observe E must shift you in the opposite direction by an exactly compensating amount. Third: therefore a non-observation is always informative whenever the observation would have been, and the absence of evidence is evidence of absence — weakly or strongly depending on how likely the cause was to produce the observation.

## My Assessment
The causal direction trip-up was the sticky part for me — I initially kept thinking of an observation as something that had to *cause* the state of the world, rather than the other way around. Once I worked through the driveway example (friend at home → car usually there → no car means update toward not-home), the Warren Fifth Column case became obvious and the machinery of the three posts snapped into place. The most useful take-away is the operational test: when I notice myself treating "we haven't seen X yet" as *confirming* a hypothesis that predicts X, I'm making Warren's error. The "strength of a model is what it prohibits" line is the one I expect to use most often — it reframes model quality from explanatory scope to anticipatory constraint.
