# Planning and attention precede consciousness in evolutionary terms, making them necessary but not sufficient conditions for it

Source: [[a-world-appears-a-journey-into-consciousness-thread|A World Appears: A Journey into Consciousness]]

A common move in everyday definitions of consciousness is to fold cognitive capacities like planning, attention control, and counterfactual reasoning into consciousness itself — to say that to be conscious is, at least in part, to plan, to attend, to imagine alternatives. Friston's evolutionary staircase explicitly rejects this folding. On his account, planning emerges several rungs below consciousness; mammals plan, and most mammals are not (or are only marginally) conscious in the rich sense humans are. Attention control emerges later than planning but still below consciousness proper. Consciousness itself appears only when social complexity demands recursive modeling of other minds.

This means that capacities often included in the definition of consciousness are, on this view, necessary but not sufficient. You cannot have human consciousness without planning, but you can have planning without human consciousness. The same holds for attention. The implication is that any definition of consciousness that simply lists "planning + attention + reflection + …" is overincluding — it captures systems that have the capacities without the conscious dimension the definition is trying to pick out.

The practical use of this point is diagnostic. When evaluating whether some system (a particular animal, a synthetic system) is conscious, asking "does it plan?" or "does it have attention?" is the wrong question. Those capacities tell you it is somewhere on the staircase but do not tell you it has cleared the rung where consciousness emerges. The right question, on this account, is whether the system is doing the kind of inference that consciousness was selected to do — recursive modeling of other minds in a socially complex environment.

**Open questions:**
- If planning and attention are necessary but not sufficient, what additional capacity is the sufficient one — and is "social inference" as a sufficient condition itself in danger of overinclusion (e.g., systems that model other minds without homeostatic stakes)?
- Are there definitions of consciousness that survive this diagnostic, or does any definition that lists capacities risk overinclusion?
