# People often demand extraordinary evidence for ordinary claims when the claim is inconvenient rather than unlikely

Source: [[a-first-look-at-bayes|A First Look at Bayes]]

Motivated skepticism is the error of raising the evidence bar for a claim not because the claim has low prior probability, but because accepting the claim would be personally inconvenient. The rule is "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence," not "inconvenient but ordinary claims require extraordinary evidence."

A manager says a new hire is not working out. The base rate for a bad hire is roughly 1 in 4 — this is an ordinary claim with modest prior odds against it. A manager's direct report, given the base rate, provides a likelihood ratio around 10:1, which is more than enough to make the posterior strongly favor the claim being true. Yet someone who recommended that hire might demand documented proof, recorded incidents, or formal performance reviews — evidence appropriate for an extraordinary claim, not an ordinary one.

The Bayesian lens makes this error visible: the person is not actually uncertain about the claim's probability. They are treating an ordinary prior as if it were extreme because accepting the evidence would require them to act on an uncomfortable conclusion. Recognizing this pattern — asking "am I demanding more evidence because the claim is unlikely, or because I don't want it to be true?" — is one of the most practically useful applications of Bayesian thinking in everyday reasoning.
