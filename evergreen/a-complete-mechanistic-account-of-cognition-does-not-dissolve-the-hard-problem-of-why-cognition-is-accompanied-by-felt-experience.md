# A complete mechanistic account of cognition does not dissolve the hard problem of why cognition is accompanied by felt experience

Source: [[a-world-appears-a-journey-into-consciousness-thread|A World Appears: A Journey into Consciousness]]

Frameworks like Karl Friston's free energy principle aim to explain cognition mechanistically, all the way down. Under such a framework, hunger is a prediction-error signal generated by interoceptive inference; food-seeking is the action that minimizes the predicted error; the entire chain from sensation to motivation to behavior can in principle be specified in terms of inference, prediction, and surprise minimization. The mechanistic story is, in this sense, complete — it leaves no step where "and then a mind decides" needs to be inserted.

But this mechanistic completeness does not explain why the mechanism is accompanied by felt experience rather than running silently. A thermostat minimizes surprise relative to a setpoint; a hungry animal minimizes surprise relative to its homeostatic priors. The animal feels hungry. The thermostat (presumably) does not. A complete mechanistic specification of the animal's hunger-driven behavior does not, by itself, predict the felt urgency that accompanies it. The hard problem of consciousness — why there is something it is like to be in a given cognitive state — is not dissolved by the mechanistic account; it is recovered at the level of phenomenology.

This is a diagnostic worth holding when reading mechanistic theories of mind. A theory may be a beautiful account of *what processes drive action* and still tell you nothing about *why the processes are accompanied by experience*. The two questions are separable, and progress on the first does not constitute progress on the second. To dismiss the hard problem because the mechanistic story looks complete is to confuse the answer to one question for the answer to another.

The implication for the Free Energy Principle and similar unifying frameworks is not that they are wrong, but that their reach is bounded. They explain the bookkeeping of life — how systems persist, what drives action, why behavior tracks survival — without explaining the felt dimension that makes some of that bookkeeping conscious.

**Open questions:**
- Is the hard problem genuine (consciousness is something *additional* to the mechanism), or is it an artifact of how we ask the question (the felt dimension just *is* what the mechanism is from the inside, and the apparent gap is a confusion)?
- What kind of evidence would distinguish those two positions?
