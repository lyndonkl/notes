# Intelligence scales through social organization not individual cognitive upgrades

Source: [[agentic-ai-and-the-next-intelligence-explosion|Agentic AI and the Next Intelligence Explosion]]

Every major leap in intelligence — from primates to language-using humans to institution-building civilizations — was driven not by upgrades to individual cognitive hardware, but by new forms of social organization that enabled collective knowledge creation and accumulation. Primate intelligence scaled with social group size, not habitat difficulty. Human language created what Tomasello calls the "cultural ratchet": knowledge accumulating across generations without any single individual needing to reconstruct the whole. Writing, law, and bureaucracy externalized intelligence into infrastructure that coordinated across longer time horizons than any participant within it.

This pattern suggests that the complexity of problems a group can solve scales primarily with its capacity to collaborate, not with the raw power of any individual member. A Sumerian scribe running a grain accounting system could not comprehend its macroeconomic function, yet the system was functionally more intelligent than he was. Intelligence, on this view, is better understood as a property of systems and networks than of individual minds.

The implication for AI is direct: if intelligence has always been a social phenomenon, then the path to more powerful AI runs through composing richer social systems of agents — not through building a single colossal oracle.

**Open questions:**
- Is social organization truly the *primary* driver of intelligence scaling, or is it one factor among several (including individual cognitive capacity, environmental pressure, and tool use)? The claim is strong and the evidence is analogical rather than causal.
- At what point does the analogy between human social intelligence and AI agent collaboration break down? Human social cognition evolved over millions of years with embodied, situated agents — AI agents operate under fundamentally different constraints.
