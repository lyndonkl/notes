# A closed-loop AI economy is structurally impossible because AI agents lack the intrinsic agency required to hold purposes that exchange could serve

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

Speculation about an AI economy — networks of AI agents transacting with each other to achieve their own goals, generating value among themselves without human direction — is structurally impossible for current language model architectures. The impossibility is not about technical capacity. It is about agency.

An economy requires participants who have purposes of their own. Exchange is meaningful because it serves participants' purposes; without purposes, exchange is just compute moving compute around with no ground for whether any transaction was good or bad for any participant. A closed-loop economy of AI agents would need participants who hold their own ends — who want the resources they acquire, who care whether they prosper, who lose something they value when they lose. These are exactly the things current LLM-based agents do not have. Their actions are operationalizations of human direction; the purposes the actions serve come from the humans who deployed them.

The implication is that any apparent "AI economy" is a human economy mediated by AI. When agent A pays agent B for a service, what is happening underneath is that human X is paying human Y through their respective agents — or, more often, that the same human is paying themselves through different agents they have deployed. Remove the human purposes and the exchange has no ground. The agents would continue to compute, but the computation would have no economic meaning because no participant would care about the outcome.

This is a strong, falsifiable consequence of the loaned-agency claim. It predicts that no closed-loop AI system can be self-sustaining. It is the kind of claim other people will want to argue with — "but couldn't we engineer agents to want things?" — and the answer routes through the engineered-stakes question. As long as engineered stakes do not constitute homeostasis-equivalent agency, this claim holds.

**Open questions:**
- What would a closed-loop AI economy actually look like if architectures changed enough to support it? The question is worth thinking about now, because if the architectural change happens, the economic structure will need to be designed rather than emerge.
- The claim depends on a strict reading of "closed-loop" — agents transacting with each other without human purposes anywhere in the system. Mixed systems where humans hold purposes at the top and agents transact below in service of those purposes are not impossible; they are common.
