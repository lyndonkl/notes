# The human-agent loop is a permanent architecture for agentic AI not a transitional stage

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

The dominant assumption in agentic AI discourse is that current human-in-loop arrangements are temporary scaffolding to be removed as model capability improves. The framework developed across these notes implies the opposite. As long as the underlying architecture lacks consequential agency, the human is not in the loop because the agent isn't yet good enough — the human is in the loop because the agent has no stake in whether the work is good.

The implication is structural rather than pragmatic. Improving the agent's analytical capability does not push it closer to autonomy on the dimension that matters. Closer-to-perfect analysis still terminates at the act-of-decision moment, where someone with skin in the game has to decide whether to act on the analysis. That role does not become smaller as the agent improves; it stays exactly where it was, because the role exists to supply the agency the agent does not have.

This reframes what "improvement" of agentic AI means. The most valuable design work is not making agents that need less supervision. It is making the human-agent loop itself more efficient, more legible, more conducive to the kinds of human work that agency-supply requires (evaluation, judgment, accountability). The loop is the architecture, not a transitional stage of it.

**Open questions:**
- What does well-designed loop architecture look like? The current literature treats "human in the loop" as a single design choice rather than a design space with its own structure.
- If engineered stakes can give agents real consequential agency, this claim weakens proportionally. The claim's strength is conditional on the engineered-stakes question being resolved against engineered consequences counting.
