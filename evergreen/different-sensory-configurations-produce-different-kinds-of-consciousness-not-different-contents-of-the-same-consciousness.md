# Different sensory configurations produce different kinds of consciousness, not different contents of the same consciousness

Source: [[a-world-appears-a-journey-into-consciousness-thread|A World Appears: A Journey into Consciousness]]

When we ask how a bat's consciousness differs from a human's, the trivial answer is that the contents differ — a bat senses through echolocation, a human through vision, so the streams of information differ. But there is a stronger claim available, and it is the one worth defending: the specific configuration of senses and the consciousness they produce are not separable. There is no generic, configuration-independent consciousness that gets parameterized by which sensors plug into it. A bat's consciousness is constitutively echolocation-driven; a human's is constitutively the particular blend of sight, hearing, smell, proprioception, interoception, and the rest. Configuration and conscious character are inseparable.

This is stronger than the trivial claim because it denies that "consciousness" is a portable variable. You cannot meaningfully ask what your consciousness would be like with a bat's senses, because the entity having that consciousness would not be you — it would be a different kind of conscious thing. The configuration constitutes the conscious entity rather than parameterizing some underlying generic experience.

The claim has empirical traction. Sensory substitution research (Bach-y-Rita's work onward) shows that when novel sensory inputs are routed to the brain — camera input through a tongue grid, for example — the brain rewires and produces new perceptual experience. Developmental cortical rewiring studies show that cortical regions adapt to whatever input flows into them. The brain is plastic to its inputs, and the resulting experience is constituted by those inputs, not by some Platonic experiential machinery sitting behind the sensors and translating their signals.

This view aligns with Thomas Nagel's classic argument: if a bat's consciousness is constitutively echolocation-driven, then asking "what it is like to be a bat" is not a content question that could in principle be reconstructed by listing inputs. It is a constitutive question about a different kind of conscious entity altogether — one whose conscious character is inseparable from its specific configuration.

**Open questions:**
- Does "configuration" include interoceptive signals (gut-brain axis, hormonal state, proprioception, somatic markers), or only exteroception? The strong claim probably says yes — all signals are constitutive — but worth pinning down.
- If configuration is constitutive, in what sense do two humans share "the same" consciousness? Is each individual's specific developmental and bodily configuration constitutive of an individual kind of conscious thing?
