# The ultimate feedback signal in agentic AI is human subjective satisfaction not objective outcome

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

When an agent system performs work — runs an analysis, executes a trade, drafts a piece of code, makes a recommendation — the question of whether the work was successful does not bottom out in the objective outcome of the work. It bottoms out in whether the human who directed the work is satisfied with what was produced.

Even when an agent's action produces a measurable result that could in principle close the loop directly — a trade that made money, code that passed tests, a forecast that turned out correct — the operationally meaningful feedback for the agent's continued use is the human's evaluation of those results. A profitable trade by an agent acting on a thesis the human disagrees with does not vindicate the agent. A losing trade taken on a thesis the human endorses, with appropriate analysis, may not condemn it. The signal that closes the loop is upstream of the outcome and lives in the human's assessment of whether the work was the right work.

This follows from the fact that agency is loaned. An agent's actions are operationalizations of human intention; the criterion for whether those actions succeeded is therefore the criterion of the human whose intention is being operationalized. There is no bypass route through objective outcomes that lets the loop close without the human's satisfaction as the terminal signal.

**Open questions:**
- This implies that the design of agent systems must take human satisfaction as a first-class feedback signal. What does that look like architecturally — beyond chat-style approval — when agents operate over long horizons or in complex teams?
- What happens when the human's stated satisfaction diverges from their actual interests (sycophancy, surface-pleasing output, evaluator inattention)? The feedback signal is fragile in proportion to the human's evaluative skill.
