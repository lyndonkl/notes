# The gap between language model output and complete work lives at the act-of-decision moment not the analysis moment

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

Language models can survey a problem space, run simulations, generate forecasts, and produce analyses with skill that approximates or exceeds many human specialists. The gap that requires human effort is not located in this analysis layer. It is located one step downstream, at the moment of acting on the analysis.

In running predictions on cricket contracts via Kalshi, my agent teams competently extract player form, run Monte Carlo simulations across game phases, compute edge against market price, and assess variance. But when the edge is small, when the stakes are personal, or when the choice is structurally subjective, no amount of additional analysis from the model produces the action. The human has to decide whether to take the position. The same pattern repeats in fantasy sports lineup decisions, in investment allocation, and in deciding whether to merge a pull request: analysis is delegable; the decision to act is not.

This locates the limit of agentic AI more precisely than "it can't do everything." The model does not stop at the edge of analytical capability. It stops at the edge of whose accountability is at stake. For decisions where the consequences fall on me, I find I am unwilling to delegate the act itself, regardless of how strong the analysis behind it is.

**Open questions:**
- Is this an irreducible boundary or a temporary one? My current intuition is irreducible, grounded in the consequential-agency argument, but the engineered-stakes question complicates this.
- Are there domains where the act-of-decision moment is less personally stake-laden, and if so, do those domains see closer-to-full automation? If yes, the boundary is less about AI capability and more about the structure of accountability.
