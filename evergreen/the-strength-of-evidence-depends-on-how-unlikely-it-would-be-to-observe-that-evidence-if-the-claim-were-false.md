# The strength of evidence depends on how unlikely it would be to observe that evidence if the claim were false

Source: [[a-first-look-at-bayes|A First Look at Bayes]]

In the likelihood ratio — probability of evidence given the claim is true, divided by probability of evidence given the claim is false — it is the denominator that drives evidential strength. The numerator matters less because most evidence is reasonably likely if the claim is true. What makes evidence powerful is when it would almost never show up in a world where the claim is false.

A blurry photo of something brown and jumping is weak evidence for a kangaroo in Mumbai because you could easily see blurry brown things without any kangaroo — the denominator is high. A clear video with the house number and a neighbor's confirmation is strong evidence because that combination would almost never appear unless there really were a kangaroo — the denominator is tiny. Both score similarly on the numerator; the denominator is what separates them.

This means that when evaluating evidence, the key question is not "is this what I'd expect to see if the claim is true?" but rather "could I easily explain seeing this if the claim were false?" If the answer to the second question is yes, the evidence is weak regardless of how consistent it is with the claim being true.
