# Systems knowledge is what converts cross-domain literacy into reusable agent infrastructure that can act on it

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

Cross-domain reading produces knowledge but not capability. A reader can absorb the principles of corporate valuation, of behavioral economics, of sports analytics, and still have nothing that operationalizes that knowledge in their daily life. What converts the absorbed knowledge into capability is systems knowledge: the engineering practice of taking a body of domain reasoning and encoding it as composable units — skills, tools, data sources, agents — that can be invoked, combined, and tested.

This systems knowledge transfers from software engineering. The skill of identifying a recurrent pattern, extracting it as a reusable abstraction, and giving it a clean interface is the same move regardless of whether the abstraction is a function in a programming language or a skill in an agent stack. Engineers who have built infrastructure or worked across multiple systems already have this skill. What's new is applying it to *agent infrastructure as a product category*: where the abstractions are skills and roles rather than classes and modules, and where the substrate being composed is text rather than code.

The implication is that systems knowledge is the mediating capability between two states: knowing a lot about a side domain, and being able to do useful work in that domain via agent composition. Without systems knowledge, side-domain reading remains potential. With it, the reading becomes infrastructure.

**Open questions:**
- Does systems knowledge have to come from software engineering, or can it be developed from other systems-design backgrounds (organizational design, operations research, scientific instrument design)? If alternatives exist, the AI-native operator profile is more accessible than it currently appears.
- Is there a way to teach systems knowledge directly, or does it require the kind of hands-on exposure that comes from years of building real systems?
