# Testing a belief against reality requires operational criteria, not intuitive plausibility

Source: [[map-territory-rationality|Map-Territory Rationality]]

Plausibility is a feeling. It's the sensation of "this could be true" or "this sounds right," a sense that your belief coheres with other things you think you know. It's also almost useless as a guide to reality. Beliefs that feel plausible are often wrong. Beliefs that feel implausible can be accurate. The map is not the territory, and plausibility is a property of the map, not the territory.

To test whether a belief actually corresponds to reality, you need operational criteria. These are questions about what you would *observe* or *measure* if your belief were true versus false. Not what you would intuitively expect, not what seems reasonable by analogy with things you've experienced before. What specific observations would prove you wrong?

This is the difference between asking "Is this theory plausible?" and asking "What would this theory predict that I could actually test?" A theory that predicts the same observations whether true or false is not testable, and untestable theories are not beliefs about reality—they're untethered decorations on your thinking. But a theory that says "if this is true, I would measure X, and if it's false, I would measure Y" is making a claim about the territory, not just about your map.

Operational criteria are the bridge between thought and reality. They force you to be specific about what truth would look like. They prevent you from hiding behind abstract language or intuitive appeal. They also discipline you against moving the goalposts: if your criterion is clear before the test, you can't reinterpret the results to match your prior belief.

This is harder than it sounds. Our brains naturally prefer the comfort of plausibility to the rigor of operational testing. It takes effort to translate a belief into testable claims. But that effort is exactly what separates genuine beliefs about the world from sophisticated confabulation.
