# The strength of a model is defined by what it prohibits, not by what it explains

Source: [[yudkowsky-bayesian-evidence-trilogy|Yudkowsky — Bayesian Evidence Trilogy]]

A model that can explain any outcome whatsoever contains no information about the world, because it has failed to distribute probability unevenly across possible observations. Explanation power is cheap: anyone can produce a story for why something happened after it happened. The expensive and valuable property is *constraint* — the model's willingness to say "this outcome would not happen" before the outcome is known. Only prohibitions constrain anticipation, and only anticipation can be tested.

This extends the individual-belief version of the principle (a belief that predicts identical experiences whether true or false is not a belief about reality) to the level of entire models and theories. A conspiracy theory that reinterprets every possible observation as further proof is maximally unfalsifiable and therefore maximally empty — equivalent, epistemically, to having no model and no eyes at all. The same failure mode shows up in milder forms whenever a theory keeps acquiring auxiliary hypotheses to absorb anomalies: each patch reduces the set of observations the theory rules out, which means each patch reduces the theory's strength, even though it feels like the theory is becoming more comprehensive.

The practical diagnostic: when evaluating a model I hold, I should be able to state in advance what observations would convince me it is wrong. If no such observations exist, I am not holding a model of the world — I am holding a floating belief that happens to use the grammar of science. The correct response when I notice this is not to defend the belief but to stop holding it as knowledge about reality.
