# Systems-engineering skill of designing how teams of agents collaborate across functional domains will be the high-value engineering skill of the AI-native digital economy

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

The high-value engineering skill of the AI-native digital economy is not writing code that solves a domain-specific problem in isolation. It is designing how teams of specialized agents collaborate across functional domains — marketing, sales, product, data analytics, design, operations — to produce outputs that no single agent or single team could produce alone.

This skill requires stepping back from the narrow domain in which a software engineer typically operates. To design agents that span the cross-functional value chain, you have to see how the chain works: how marketing produces leads that sales converts, how product decisions feed data analytics that informs design, how the multiple teams' outputs interact through shared metrics and funnels. Most software engineers have not had to develop this perspective because their value was historically generated within a specialty. AI-native engineering requires the perspective.

This is, structurally, organizational science applied at the agent layer. A century of research on how human teams collaborate, divide labor, resolve disagreement, and aggregate output across functions becomes directly applicable to designing agent systems. The engineers who can do this work are the ones who can hold the 3000-foot picture and design coordination patterns across it. Not every software engineer can; the ones who can will be disproportionately valuable.

**Open questions:**
- Is this skill teachable, or does it emerge from years of cross-functional exposure that most engineering careers don't provide?
- Could the skill itself be partly automated — agents designing agent teams — or does it remain a fundamentally human capability because only humans hold the purposes the agent teams serve?
