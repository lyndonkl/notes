# Epistemic rationality and instrumental rationality are distinct failure modes

Source: [[map-territory-rationality|Map-Territory Rationality]]

You can build a perfectly accurate map of reality and still fail to achieve your goals. You can have a thoroughly mistaken map and succeed through luck or the inherent tolerance of your environment. This is because there are two separable dimensions of rationality, and they require different diagnostics.

Epistemic rationality is the domain of the map-maker. It's about building beliefs that correspond to reality, about constraining your mental model so that it predicts experience accurately. You're trying to know the territory. The tests are calibration, predictive accuracy, operational criteria that distinguish true beliefs from false ones. You can fail at epistemic rationality by being overconfident, by ignoring base rates, by updating too little on evidence.

Instrumental rationality is the domain of the actor. It's about taking actions that achieve your goals, whatever those goals are. You might have a completely wrong map of how the world works, but if you act in ways that happen to be effective despite your ignorance, you've succeeded instrumentally. Conversely, you might understand reality perfectly and still fail to achieve what you want because you don't know how to translate knowledge into action, or because your goals are working against each other, or because you're simply not effective at execution.

The confusion between these two comes naturally. We say "rationality" as if it's one thing, but it's not. A scientist can be epistemically rigorous while being instrumentally terrible at politics. A politician can be instrumentally masterful while peddling false beliefs. A conspiracy theorist can be instrumentally brilliant at recruiting followers while being epistemically catastrophic at distinguishing signal from noise.

What matters for your own success is recognizing which one is actually broken when you fail. If you're not achieving your goals despite understanding the problem correctly, your failure is instrumental: you need to fix your execution, your strategy, your priorities. If you're confused about how the world works, no amount of effective action will help—you need epistemic repair first. Fixing the wrong dimension wastes effort.
