# Feelings have evolutionary function because their valence translates prediction error into homeostasis-restoring action

Source: [[a-world-appears-a-journey-into-consciousness-thread|A World Appears: A Journey into Consciousness]]

The Friston framework gives an account of why life minimizes uncertainty, but it does not, on its own, explain why some uncertainty-minimizing systems *feel* their predictions failing while others (presumably thermostats) do not. Evan Thompson's contribution is to make valence the bridge. Feelings are not arbitrary side effects of cognition; they are always positively or negatively valenced, and the valence is not a free parameter — it tracks the direction of uncertainty change. Reduction in uncertainty produces positive valence; increase in uncertainty produces negative valence.

This makes feelings evolutionarily indispensable rather than ornamental. A system that registers prediction error without *feeling* it has nothing pulling it toward correction; a system that feels prediction error has built-in motivational pressure to act. Valence is how the prediction-error signal becomes behavior. The organism that feels its hunger acts to reduce it; the organism that merely tracks declining blood sugar without feeling does not. Feelings are the mechanism by which homeostatic violation gets translated into homeostasis-restoring action.

Stated this way, feelings are not optional features of advanced cognition. They are part of the engine that explains how uncertainty-minimization scales up the evolutionary staircase at all. Each new rung — memory, planning, attention, social inference — would offer no selective advantage without something to motivate the system to use it. Valence supplies that motivation. Without valence, the staircase has the structural capacity to climb but no force pulling it upward.

The price tag of this view is that it commits you to attributing some primitive valenced experience to any system whose behavior is shaped by prediction error in a goal-directed way. If your line for "where feelings begin" is drawn higher than that, you owe an account of what fills the motivational role at the lower rungs.

**Open questions:**
- What is the simplest system to which this view would attribute valenced experience — single-celled organisms, plants, animals only? On what principled basis is the line drawn?
- Could a non-biological system implement a valence-like mechanism (something that translates prediction error into action with built-in motivational force) without having "feelings" in the morally-relevant sense? If yes, the valence-feelings identity is not tight.
