# Scalable AI alignment requires institutional templates not individual virtue

Source: [[agentic-ai-and-the-next-intelligence-explosion|Agentic AI and the Next Intelligence Explosion]]

The dominant paradigm for AI alignment — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) — resembles a parent-child model of correction: a single human evaluator shaping a single model's behavior. This is fundamentally dyadic and cannot scale to systems involving billions of interacting agents. If the future of AI involves vast networks of human and artificial agents collaborating, the alignment problem cannot be solved one agent at a time.

The alternative is institutional alignment: designing persistent templates of roles, norms, and protocols that govern agent behavior regardless of which specific agent fills a given role. Just as a courtroom functions because "judge," "attorney," and "jury" are well-defined slots independent of who occupies them, scalable AI systems could be governed by institutional structures — courtrooms, markets, bureaucracies — that define acceptable behavior through role protocols rather than individual training.

This reframes the alignment challenge from "how do we make each AI agent good?" to "how do we design systems where agents behave well because of the structural incentives and constraints they operate within?" It shifts the focus from individual virtue to institutional design — from character to architecture.

**Open questions:**
- What happens when institutional templates fail? Human institutions regularly produce corruption, regulatory capture, and perverse incentives. How would "agent institutions" handle these failure modes?
- Is there a hybrid approach where individual alignment (RLHF-style) provides a baseline and institutional alignment handles coordination at scale?
- The paper asserts this is necessary but provides no concrete examples of what an "agent institution" would look like in practice. What would a minimal working prototype involve?
