# For every expectation of evidence there is an equal and opposite expectation of counterevidence

Source: [[yudkowsky-bayesian-evidence-trilogy|Yudkowsky — Bayesian Evidence Trilogy]]

The rule follows directly from the law of total probability: P(H) = P(H|E)·P(E) + P(H|¬E)·P(¬E). Your current belief P(H) is already the weighted average of where you would end up after seeing E and where you would end up after not seeing E. This means the mere act of planning to look cannot, on average, move your belief in any particular direction — if it could, you would already be there. The expected posterior equals the prior, always.

The concrete form of the constraint is a sign-symmetry: if observing E would push your belief toward H, then observing ¬E must push it away from H, by an amount weighted so that the two expected shifts exactly cancel. You cannot design an experiment, interpretation, or argument that expects only confirming updates — every plan for confirmation is simultaneously a plan for disconfirmation of equal expected magnitude. This is why scientists pre-register predictions: it makes both branches of the accusation tree visible at once, which is the only way to prevent the Spee-style failure where you see only the branch that convicted the witch.

The corollary that matters in practice: for a true Bayesian, there is no such thing as seeking evidence to *confirm* a theory. You can only seek evidence to *test* one. Any experimental design that promises to make confidence go up on expectation is either incoherent or secretly a plan to decrease confidence elsewhere by the same amount.
