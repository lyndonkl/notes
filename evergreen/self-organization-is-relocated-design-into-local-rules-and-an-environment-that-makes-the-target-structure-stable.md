# Self-organization is not the absence of design but its relocation into local rules and an environment that makes the target structure the stable configuration

Source: [[mit-7016-introductory-biology-thread|MIT 7.016 Introductory Biology (lecture series)]]

Phospholipids are amphipathic: a water-loving head and a water-hating tail. Drop a crowd of them into water and they assemble themselves into a bilayer — the boundary of every cell — with no blueprint and nothing directing the process. The arrangement is not imposed from outside; it falls out of each molecule locally minimizing its contact with water (the hydrophobic effect). Tails hide together in the middle, heads face the water on both sides.

It is tempting to call this "order for free," but that misreads it. The bilayer forms only because the environment — water — makes the organized state the energetically favorable one. Take the water away and you get a greasy puddle, not a membrane. So self-organization is not the absence of design; it is design *relocated*. The design moves out of an explicit, step-by-step blueprint and into two places: the local properties each component carries (amphipathic by construction), and an environment that makes the target structure the stable configuration. The environment is a co-designer, not a backdrop.

The practical consequence is that you can stop specifying a global structure directly and instead specify local rules plus an enabling environment — but only if you can identify the "force" that makes the structure you want the favorable one. Where no such force exists, nothing self-assembles. This reframes self-organization from a hope ("maybe order will emerge") into an engineering question ("what gradient makes this particular order the stable state?").

**Open questions:**
- For a given target structure, how do you find — or manufacture — the environmental "force" that makes it favorable? Is there a general method, or is it bespoke per system?
