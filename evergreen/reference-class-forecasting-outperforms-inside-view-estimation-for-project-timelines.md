# Reference class forecasting outperforms inside-view estimation for project timelines

Source: [[map-territory-rationality|Map-Territory Rationality]]

The empirical pattern is stark and reproducible. When teams estimate project timelines by reasoning from their specific circumstances—analyzing task dependencies, considering team skill levels, accounting for known risks—they produce forecasts that are systematically too optimistic. When those same projects actually complete, they routinely exceed their estimates. This is the planning fallacy: the gap between what we predict and what actually happens.

But there's a remedy that works, and it's almost comically simple: instead of building a detailed inside-view model, ask a reference class question. Identify similar projects that have already been completed. What was their actual timeline? The answer to that question—the base rate from your reference class—predicts the outcome of your new project better than your detailed, thoughtful, project-specific analysis does.

This works because the reference class captures real patterns in how long work actually takes. It absorbs all the hidden variabilities, the unforeseen delays, the ways that reality is messier than the map. It's not smarter than you; it's just less optimistic than your inside view, and that pessimism is actually calibration. When research subjects were given both their own inside-view forecast and the base rate for their reference class, the ones who trusted the base rate over their inside view produced more accurate predictions. The ones who tried to split the difference ended up somewhere in the middle—worse than base rate alone, but better than inside view alone.

The mechanism isn't mysterious. Your inside view is vulnerable to conjunction fallacy, anchoring bias, and the general human tendency to underestimate uncertainty. The reference class has already paid the price for these errors; it's already been tempered by reality. Trusting it means trusting the accumulated experience of people who have done similar work before, even though they weren't you and their projects weren't identical to yours.
