# Cellular function is a property of the collective bioelectric context, not of the cell itself

Source: [[a-world-appears-a-journey-into-consciousness-thread|A World Appears: A Journey into Consciousness]]

A cell in a developed multicellular organism is not intrinsically a skin cell or a retinal cell. Its role is being enforced by the bioelectric field of its collective. The role looks intrinsic from the outside because the field is steady and the cell holds its assignment for the organism's lifetime. But the assignment is contingent on the collective context, not constitutive of the cell.

Michael Levin's Xenobot work makes this concrete. Scrape skin cells from a tadpole, free them from their original bioelectric context, place them in a nutrient solution, and they do not stay inert. They aggregate, form a new bioelectric field linking the cluster, and adopt new functions — repurposing cilia originally meant for clearing infection into a means of locomotion. The same cells, given a different collective context, become a different kind of organism. Anthrobots, made from human tracheal cells, demonstrate that the principle is not specific to one species or tissue.

The implication is that cellular function is a property of the configuration rather than of the cell. What a cell does depends on what collective it is part of — what signals it receives, what role the bioelectric field assigns it. There is no fixed "skin cell" floating free of context; "skin cell" is a description of what a cell is currently doing in a particular bioelectric environment.

This generalizes a pattern that recurs at multiple scales: function is often configurational, not componential. A neuron in isolation is not "doing memory"; the network is. A team in isolation is not solving a problem; the organization is. The cellular case shows the same logic running below the level of organism — all the way down.

**Open questions:**
- Is bioelectric coordination the only mechanism by which cellular collectives enforce role assignment, or are there other mechanisms that achieve the same configurational effect?
- What does this view imply about cancer — described in the source as cells "ignoring" the bioelectric orders and remaining independent? Is cancer a failure of configurational enforcement, and what does that suggest about how the enforcement actually works?
