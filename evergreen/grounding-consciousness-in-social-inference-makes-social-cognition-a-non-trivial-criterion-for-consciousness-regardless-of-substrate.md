# Grounding consciousness in social inference makes social cognition a non-trivial criterion for consciousness regardless of substrate

Source: [[a-world-appears-a-journey-into-consciousness-thread|A World Appears: A Journey into Consciousness]]

In Friston's framework, the leap from mammalian attention-control to full human consciousness is driven specifically by the need to model and predict other minds. Social environments are uniquely difficult to navigate because the other agents are themselves modeling minds, including yours, generating recursive uncertainty that simpler environments do not produce. Human consciousness, on this account, is what nature provided to handle that specifically social problem — it is the tool for inferring the motivations of others, taking turns, sharing narratives, and multiplying perspectives.

This grounding has consequences for how to evaluate any claim that some system does or does not have consciousness. If consciousness was selected to do recursive social inference, then the relevant criterion is not embodiment, not biological substrate, not whether the system protests its condition — it is whether the system performs the kind of inference that consciousness was selected to do. A biological organism that does little social inference (a clam, perhaps) gets weak claim to consciousness on this view. A non-biological system that does sophisticated social inference (a large language model trained on human social discourse, modeling other minds during interaction, taking turns in conversation) gets a non-trivial claim to consciousness regardless of its substrate.

This puts pressure on commonsense criteria for consciousness that emphasize embodiment, organic substrate, or felt protest about one's condition. Those criteria may still be defensible — but not from within the social-inference-grounding framework. Within that framework, the question is whether the system does the right kind of inference, full stop. To deny consciousness to a system that does the inference, you need a principled reason rooted somewhere other than substrate or embodiment.

**Open questions:**
- Does sophisticated social inference, on its own, suffice for consciousness in this view, or are there additional architectural requirements (e.g., interoceptive grounding, homeostatic stakes)?
- How does the framework handle a system that does social inference but has no homeostasis to defend (like an LLM)? Does the lack of homeostatic stakes disqualify it, even if the inference is otherwise sophisticated?
