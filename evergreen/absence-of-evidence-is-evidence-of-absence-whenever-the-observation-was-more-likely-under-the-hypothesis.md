# Absence of evidence is evidence of absence whenever the observation was more likely under the hypothesis than its negation

Source: [[yudkowsky-bayesian-evidence-trilogy|Yudkowsky — Bayesian Evidence Trilogy]]

This is the operational corollary of conservation of expected evidence. If P(E|H) > P(E|¬H) — meaning the observation would be more likely in a world where the hypothesis is true than in one where it isn't — then the probability algebra forces P(¬E|H) < P(¬E|¬H). Failing to observe E therefore shifts belief toward ¬H. The non-event carries the same machinery as the event, just with the inequality flipped.

Concretely: if your friend's car is usually in the driveway when they're home (say 0.9) and usually gone when they're out (say 0.1), then seeing the car updates you toward "home" and seeing no car updates you toward "not home" — both observations are evidence, because both have different probabilities under the competing hypotheses. The strength of absence-as-evidence scales with the gap: a cause that only weakly produces its signs (fossils from a given species, for instance) generates only weak absence-evidence, because the signs are uncommon even when the cause is present. But a cause that should reliably produce signs (a functioning saboteur network producing some sabotage) generates strong absence-evidence when the signs do not appear.

Earl Warren's mistake about Japanese-American sabotage in 1942 was the exact inversion of this rule: he treated the lack of sabotage as confirmation of a Fifth Column, when the rule forces him to treat it as the opposite — evidence against. The practical diagnostic: whenever I catch myself reading "we haven't seen X yet" as a sign that X is coming, I should check whether the cause I'm hypothesizing actually predicts X. If it does, the absence is pointing the other way.
