# Specialization as the cognitive habit produced by industrial-era automation blinds people to the power of a general-purpose tool

Source: [[thought-session-what-the-g-in-agi-actually-means|Thought Session: What the G in AGI Actually Means]]

The professional culture that emerged from the Industrial Revolution organized work around specialization. Each role narrowed to a particular kind of expertise, supported by particular tools, evaluated by particular metrics. A century of this organization produced not just a labor structure but a cognitive habit: the assumption that doing serious work means going deep in one domain and trusting other domains to other specialists.

This habit is the deeper cause of why a general-purpose tool now reads as inadequate to most professionals. To perceive the value of a general tool requires a posture in which breadth is genuinely valuable — in which knowing a respectable amount about several domains is a form of capability rather than dilettantism. The cognitive habit of specialization marks that posture as suspect. The specialist's implicit question — *but how deep does it go in MY domain* — is exactly the wrong question for a tool whose value lies in spanning many domains at moderate depth.

This explains why the AGI claim feels overheated to people who, by the standards of their own field, are sophisticated. They are not failing to evaluate. They are evaluating correctly using the wrong evaluative frame — the frame the previous century of professional life trained them into, and that the tool they are now evaluating has rendered partially obsolete.

**Open questions:**
- Is this a permanent feature of professional culture, or a transient one that the next generation of professionals will be raised out of?
- Are there cultures or eras where generalism was the dominant posture (Renaissance polymaths, traditional craft cultures), and if so, did those cultures perceive general tools differently when they encountered them?
