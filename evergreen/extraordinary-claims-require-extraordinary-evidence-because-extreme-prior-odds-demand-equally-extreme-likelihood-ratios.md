# Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence because extreme prior odds demand equally extreme likelihood ratios

Source: [[a-first-look-at-bayes|A First Look at Bayes]]

The Sagan maxim "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence" is not just a rhetorical slogan — it is a direct consequence of Bayesian mechanics. An extraordinary claim is one with extreme prior odds against it, like 1:1,000,000,000 for aliens delivering bookcases. To move from those prior odds to a posterior where the claim becomes plausible, you need a likelihood ratio of comparable magnitude.

Even strong evidence — say, a 50:1 likelihood ratio from seeing a new bookcase appear overnight — barely dents a billion-to-one prior. The math is simple multiplication: 1:1,000,000,000 times 50:1 gives you 50:1,000,000,000, which is still overwhelmingly against the claim. You would need evidence with a billion-to-one likelihood ratio to reach even odds.

This formalizes something important: "extraordinary" is not about how weird the claim sounds or how uncomfortable it makes you feel. It is about the prior odds. A claim that contradicts well-established causal understanding has extreme priors against it. A claim that follows from known mechanisms — even if surprising in magnitude — has ordinary priors and needs only ordinary evidence.
