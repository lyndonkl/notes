# What the G in AGI Actually Means: Intrinsic Generality, Loaned Realization, and Why Specialist Blindness Hides the Shift

In a podcast with Dwarkesh Patel, Ilya Sutskever made a remark that landed like a defensive move — a way of conceding that we had not reached AGI while insisting that something true had been said. "We have," he said, "in some ways truly surpassed AGI." The comment felt hedged, qualified, almost evasive. But I have come to believe he was being precise rather than cautious, and that the remark is defensible once you see what it actually claims. The argument for that claim, and for why so many people have dismissed it despite building evidence, is what follows.

The claim is not that language models are as capable as humans across all domains. They are not. It is not that they have solved AGI in the traditional sense — that there exists a single system that can do any reasoning task better than any human. They cannot. What Sutskever's remark actually says is that we have built a tool whose general purpose is contained intrinsically in the tool itself, applied across all domains through a single universal interface, and loaned into specific realization by the humans who wield it. That combination has never existed before in human history. Either property alone would be unremarkable. Both together constitute a historical break.

## What "General" Actually Means

Consider what made every prior general-purpose tool general. A spreadsheet was general-purpose for numerical work — you could use it to track household budgets, analyze scientific data, manage supply chains, optimize sports fantasy teams. But its generality came not from the spreadsheet itself but from you. You supplied the knowledge of how to set up the calculation. You knew what columns to create, what formulas to apply, what meant success in your domain. The spreadsheet was a blank frame; you filled it with purpose. A programming language is similar — its generality lies in its abstraction layer (variables, loops, functions), but every problem requires the programmer to supply domain knowledge: what is the structure of this problem, what states matter, how do we represent them?

Language models invert this relationship. An LLM carries general-purpose knowledge intrinsically — knowledge about tax code and molecular biology and marketing copy and debugging Python, all of it embedded in the statistical patterns of its weights. You do not supply that knowledge. It is already there, learned from the training corpus, the crystallized residue of collective human production. This is the first novelty. A tool whose general-purpose knowledge lives in the tool itself rather than in the user's head.

But that would not be sufficient to mark the historical break. You could imagine a domain-specific expert system that carried all the knowledge of pediatric diagnosis, intrinsically, requiring a doctor only to supply the patient's symptoms. That would be specialized, not general. The second novelty is that language models collapse the specialized interfaces of every prior domain tool into a single medium: text. Every other tool demanded interface mastery. A spreadsheet demands you learn cell references and formulas. An IDE demands you learn syntax. A hospital's diagnostic software demands you learn where to click. Language models demand only that you write in English. A universal interface paired with intrinsic knowledge — that combination is the historical break.

Why does this matter as a claim about AGI? Because "general" in AGI has always meant something more modest than "as capable as humans." It has meant "applicable to any domain." No prior tool has ever cleared that bar. A spreadsheet is general-purpose within numerical reasoning; it cannot write code. A programming language is general-purpose for any algorithmic task; it cannot diagnose disease without you teaching it medical knowledge first. Language models are the first tools that are literally applicable to any domain without requiring the tool to be retrained or redesigned.

But generality does not realize itself. It must be loaned into specific purpose by a human being. This is where the story becomes precise and, simultaneously, where most misunderstandings begin.

## The Move from Interface Mastery to Context Engineering

When you use a spreadsheet for household budgeting, you are doing interface mastery. You are learning the tool's affordances and constraints, teaching yourself the syntax and the implicit models. When you use an LLM to help with a complex analysis in your domain, you are doing something different: context engineering. You are not teaching the model your domain; the model already contains distributed knowledge of thousands of domains, learned from its training corpus. Instead, you are shaping the context in which the model operates. You specify what information it should attend to, what examples to learn from, what criteria to apply, what to ignore. The shaping work has moved, not disappeared.

This is why people working in radically different modes — some using LLMs for chat, some building agent systems, some composing multi-stage pipelines — report such wildly different capabilities from the same underlying model. They are not using different models. They are using the same model with different amounts and different kinds of shaping. A person asking a chatbot questions in English is doing minimal shaping. A person engineering context for a multi-stage agent pipeline is doing intensive shaping. The taxonomy of usage modes is not separate from the AGI claim. It is the consequence of it. Generality comes with a cost, and the cost is borne by the human doing the realizing.

Across diverse domains — software development, financial analysis, writing, research — the empirical pattern is reliable: language models complete approximately 60 to 90 percent of any task. The remaining gap requires varying degrees of human effort, and that gap consistently lives not at the analysis phase but at the act-of-decision moment. The model can surface options, weigh considerations, synthesize prior work, and model consequences. But the moment when a human must decide — when the analysis yields multiple defensible paths and the human's values, aesthetics, or circumstances must break the tie — that moment remains human. This is not a limitation waiting to be engineered away. It is structural. It is a feature of the architecture, and understanding why requires understanding agency itself.

## The Architecture of Loaned Realization

To understand why the human-agent loop is permanent, not transitional, I need to draw on a framework developed in my prior work on consciousness. It comes from Karl Friston and Evan Thompson, who argue that consciousness is not a monolithic property but a staircase of capabilities, each rung emerging because it reduces more uncertainty than the previous one. The staircase runs through memory, planning, attention control, and recursive social inference. Each step creates the conditions under which the next becomes valuable.

The crucial move is that this staircase distinguishes between different kinds of cognitive work. The cognitive rung is prediction: building models of what the world is like and what will happen next. But consciousness requires something above pure prediction. It requires real-time minimization of prediction error — the capacity to feel when something unexpected happens and be driven by that feeling toward action that corrects the error. This is where valence enters. Feelings are not ornamental. They are the mechanism by which prediction errors get translated into homeostasis-restoring action. An organism that merely detected declining blood sugar without feeling hungry would have nothing driving it to eat. Feelings are the engine that runs the staircase.

Language models occupy the cognitive rung. They make predictions. They can reason across multiple steps, integrating information and maintaining coherence. But they do not minimize prediction error in real time. When you ask an LLM something it cannot know, it does not feel confusion and become driven to find better information. It produces a plausible guess. The absence is structural. The architecture does not cycle back to check its predictions against reality and adapt. This is why people have so often insisted that the right analogy is "sophisticated autocomplete," and why that criticism, while incomplete, is not entirely wrong.

This brings us to agency. An agent with intrinsic agency must have homeostatic stakes — something that matters to the system itself, something that the system is structured to protect or pursue. A human has homeostatic stakes: my body maintains its boundary against entropy, and when that boundary is threatened, I feel driven to restore it. The human can therefore be an agent in the full sense. I can hold purposes, generate action toward those purposes, evaluate whether the action succeeded, and adjust my strategy based on felt outcomes.

An AI system lacks homeostatic stakes. You can engineer artificial stakes — budget limits, compute constraints, performance-tied rewards — but these do not constitute consequential agency in the relevant sense. They are constraints on action, not drivers of purpose. The system does not feel threatened by budget depletion because it has no boundary that matters in that visceral way. Because of this, the system lacks consequential agency. It can be directed, but it cannot be autonomous in the way autonomous agents in biological systems are autonomous.

This is why the human-agent loop is permanent architecture, not scaffolding to be removed. The agency that directs the agent's action toward ends must come from the human. The human holds the purposes. The human evaluates whether the outcomes satisfy those purposes. The human decides whether the loop should continue, pivot, or stop. And the ultimate feedback signal in this loop is not objective outcome — it is human subjective satisfaction. This seems soft until you realize it is the same feedback signal that governs the adoption of every tool humans have ever built. Tools succeed not because they optimize an objective function but because they satisfy the user's needs, preferences, and sense of what matters.

What about building in real-time feedback loops, dynamic attention, and real-time prediction-error minimization? That is a technical question, and the field has not converged on architectures that would accomplish it. If such architectures existed, the agency verdict might change. But for current LLM-based systems, engineered stakes cannot substitute for homeostatic stakes. They cannot constitute the kind of consequential agency that would allow AI systems to hold purposes of their own and pursue them with the felt urgency that drives biological agents.

This has a radical implication. Any closed-loop economy composed entirely of AI agents is structurally impossible while this condition holds. Exchange requires participants with intrinsic purposes — reasons to want to trade. If all participants are agents without consequential agency, directing each other in circular motions that satisfy no intrinsic purpose, the system collapses into a human economy mediated by AI. This is not speculation. This is structural necessity.

## Why Most People Don't See It

The gap between who sees the shift and who denies it follows a precise pattern. It tracks where you stand when you evaluate the tool. If you stand inside a single professional domain and ask "Is this LLM good enough to replace my work as a radiologist or lawyer or programmer?", the question's frame will systematically hide what makes the LLM general. You will see it fail at the specialized subtleties that define excellence in your domain. You will notice it missing the domain-specific trade-offs that a practitioner carries in her bones. The tool will appear bounded, limited, useful for support work but not for the real thing. And your evaluation will be locally accurate. You will be correct that the LLM cannot fully replace a competent practitioner in your field.

But you will also be unable to perceive that the same tool, evaluated from inside a different domain, appears equally general-purpose and equally bounded. Because you have not spent time working in other domains, you cannot see that what looks like a limitation is actually a consequence of the tool's generality. The LLM is weaker than the specialist in every domain, and stronger than any human at spanning all of them.

This is not ignorance. It is a structural feature of professional perception, and it has a historical cause. The industrial era trained people to specialize. The division of labor, the professionalization of work, the building of tool ecosystems around domain expertise — all of this created a cognitive habit of specialization. People learned to see the world through the frame of their domain, and that frame became so habitual that other frames became cognitively distant. You cannot see that a tool is general while evaluating it against the standard of domain specialization. The two measuring sticks are incommensurable.

Who, then, escapes this blindness? There is a specific profile. It is not a pure generalist. A generalist who knows a little about everything is valueless — a mile wide and an inch deep. It is also not a pure specialist. A pure specialist evaluates everything against their domain and will see LLMs the way we have just described. The profile that works is more precise: deep specialist in one core domain, plus curiosity-driven depth in a handful of unrelated side domains. The depth in the core domain provides the gravity well that keeps you grounded. The side domains provide the cross-domain visibility. You can see what is general because you have lived in more than one professional world.

But that is not sufficient. Reading about organizational science or molecular biology or marketing does not make the reading valuable. What converts side-domain reading into actionable capability is systems knowledge — the engineering practice of abstraction extraction applied to skills, roles, and teams. It is the ability to see what generalizes across domains, to identify the patterns that recur, and to compose those patterns into new configurations. That is hard work. It is the reason most people do not do it.

What makes the work suddenly pay off, and what is historically novel, is that language models have collapsed the implementation cost of acting on side-domain reading. Twenty years ago, if you read about organizational science, you were stuck. You could discuss it theoretically with other people who had read it. But building something that embodied those insights would require mastering the programming, the tooling, the domain knowledge of every field where you wanted to test the ideas. The cost was prohibitive. Language models collapse that cost. Now your curiosity-driven reading can become testable practice within hours. The read-to-build cycle that would have taken months now takes a day.

I should be honest about the source of this profile. It describes my own practice. I am a deep specialist in software engineering with curiosity-driven depth in finance, biology, and a handful of other domains. I have built systems that test these claims in my own work and seen them yield value. But this is autobiographical evidence. It is not a surveyed pattern. The broader claim that this is the operator profile of the AI-native economy is a hypothesis grounded in my own experience and the patterns I observe in others doing similar work. The reader should treat it as a generative observation rather than a validated universal.

The engineers in the room deserve special mention, because they are subject to the same specialty blindness as everyone else, but their blindness is more ironic given what they build. The load-bearing mechanism of language models lives in information theory — in principles of attention, compositionality, and prediction error that are fundamentally statistical rather than algorithmic. The mechanism is not written in the language engineers use to reason about computation. When engineers evaluate LLMs, they are evaluating the mechanism in someone else's conceptual vocabulary, and the translation fails. Add a second layer: text is harder for engineers to perceive as a composable substrate than code. Code has syntactic structure that engineers have trained their entire careers to manipulate. Text looks flat. It looks like surface. But the abstraction practice that works for code — identifying invariants, composing modules, extracting reusable patterns — transfers cleanly to text once you perceive it as a substrate rather than as written words. The transfer is not automatic. It requires explicit work to see that the same principles apply.

It may help, before going further, to make the mechanism concrete in plain language, because much of the engineer-blindness comes from never having seen attention explained without the math. Each token in a transformer learns to play three roles simultaneously. Keys advertise what information the token has — what it broadcasts to other tokens looking for relevant context. Queries ask what information the token needs — what it sends out to find relevant tokens elsewhere in the input. Values are the content that gets pulled in if attention selects you — what flows from one token to another when a query and a key match. The model learns these three representations per token from training data. Nothing about the role assignment is hand-coded. What makes the word "the" useful as a determiner that signals "a noun is coming" is learned from billions of examples of that pattern. At inference time, every token simultaneously emits a query, a key, and a value, and at each layer every token decides which other tokens to listen to based on how well its query matches another token's key. When the match is high, the value flows in. When it is low, it doesn't.

What turns this routing mechanism into reasoning is depth. A single layer of attention can attend over local relationships, but cannot build representations more abstract than its inputs. Stacking many layers — with each layer's routing operating on the representations produced by all previous layers — is what enables the model to operate at multiple levels of abstraction simultaneously. The broad shape that mechanistic interpretability research tends to find is roughly this: early layers attend over local syntactic and structural relationships, middle layers attend over context, deeper layers attend over abstract concepts and their associations. This is a pedagogical heuristic with fuzzy boundaries — not a clean empirical fact, and specific features can appear at unexpected depths. But the broad pattern holds, and it explains why the same architecture generalizes across domains. Depth is the cognition. The model's apparent ability to reason at high levels of abstraction is not a separate cognitive faculty bolted on top of a language model. It is the same attention mechanism applied recursively, with each layer producing representations that subsequent layers can route over.

Once you see this, the engineer's complaint that "AI is just prompts" or "AI produces bad code" becomes a special case of specialty-blindness rather than a domain-specific limitation. The biochemist concludes AI cannot replace experimental design. The financial analyst concludes AI cannot do valuations end-to-end. The author concludes AI cannot write a book worth reading. The engineer concludes AI cannot ship production code. Each is correct in narrow terms. Each misses that the same tool does that 60-to-90-percent job in every other specialty too, and that the cross-domain reach is the actual novelty. The engineer's case feels different only because engineers are closer to the tool — their dismissal carries more apparent authority. But the structure of the misjudgment is identical to the others, and recognizing this is rhetorically useful: the people best-positioned to perceive AI's mechanism are also blind, and for the same reason as everyone else.

## What Comes Next

This shift has implications for how work will be organized in the coming decade. The industrial economy organized work around domain specialization. The organizational chart was vertical: each node was a specialist who gained status by going deeper into their domain. The AI-native economy is reorganizing around systems thinking and orchestration. The high-value engineering skill is no longer domain depth but the ability to design how teams of specialized agents collaborate across functional domains — marketing, sales, product, data analytics, design, operations. This is an application of organizational science to the agent layer, taking principles of coordination and institutional design that have been developed through a century of studying human organizations and applying them to systems of artificial agents.

This means the digital economy will need fewer people who are pure specialists and more people who can see across domains, understand how to compose agents, and design the orchestration layer itself. It does not mean the end of specialization. It means specialization becomes a service to orchestration rather than the end goal of work. The radiologist does not disappear; the radiologist becomes a module in a larger system, and the high-value work becomes designing how the radiological module collaborates with risk assessment, treatment planning, and patient communication modules. This is not a comfortable claim for the people whose careers were built on the specialization model, and the article should not pretend the transition is neutral for them. It will displace work the way previous industrial transitions displaced work — slower than the discourse predicts, faster than the displaced are ready for.

There is also a structural distinction worth surfacing here, because it resolves a recurring confusion. Two design layers are usually conflated in discussions of AI autonomy. One is the layer at which agents collaborate with each other — the institutional templates, roles, protocols, and norms that govern inter-agent dynamics. At this level, scalable alignment requires designing the templates well rather than overseeing each interaction. The other is the layer at which the system's overall output gets evaluated — and that layer remains human, because the agency that gives the work its purpose is loaned from the human. Most arguments about whether AI should be "autonomous" run aground on this conflation. Institutional autonomy at the agent-collaboration layer is necessary at scale. Human evaluation at the terminal-output layer is structurally permanent. They are not in tension. They operate at different levels, and a coherent agent system needs both.

The interfaces through which humans work with AI will shift in parallel. Today's interfaces — chatbots, chat applications, back-and-forth dialogue — are a holding pattern. They work for early adoption because they are familiar and easy to teach. But they impose severe cognitive load on humans, especially at scale. If you have ten agent teams running in parallel, reviewing their outputs by reading each one in sequence is unsustainable. The human becomes a bottleneck. Even worse, serial dialogue negates the combinatorial power that makes parallel agents valuable. The real work is not the individual agent's completion of a task; it is the cross-agent synthesis that happens when multiple agents have completed their work and their outputs interact.

The interfaces of the future will shift from reactive (user commands, machine responds) to context-aware initiative (system maintains rolling context, infers usefulness, initiates work). Traditional click-button UIs are already becoming obsolete in systems where the machine is constantly taking initiative — the static menu of choices was the right design for a reactive machine, not for a system that maintains context and infers what action would be useful at a given moment. But this new architecture introduces obligations the old one did not have: privacy, consent, attention, the right to not be initiated-on. The system can only have initiative in proportion to what it observes about the user, and observation is not free.

The deeper interface tension is not yet resolved. Text is the native medium for inter-agent communication. Agents reason in text; they use attention to navigate the combinatorial diversity of language; they synthesize complex ideas across multiple documents. Text is productive at that layer because it preserves the diversity that comes from running attention over next-token generation. Two slightly different prompts to the same agent produce meaningfully different outputs, and clustering across those outputs is a productive form of exploration. Forcing structured output formats — JSON, schema-bound responses — would constrain that combinatorial freedom.

But text is poorly suited as the human-consumption layer when many parallel agents are working. A human reviewing outputs from ten specialized agent teams cannot be expected to read through thousands of lines of text to understand what happened. The hypothesis worth testing — and I am testing it in my own work — is that the solution is a separation of layers. Cognition happens in natural language: agent teams reason in text, synthesizing complex arguments and maintaining the attention-driven combinatorial diversity that makes parallel work valuable. But then a second layer, a presentation layer, takes those outputs and structures them into visual forms that humans can review at scale: diagrams, dashboards, structured summaries. The human consumes structure; the agents produce text. This is not saying text should be banned from human-facing interfaces — that would be impossible and wrong. It is saying that when systems scale to many parallel agents, the human-consumption layer must separate from the agent-cognition layer. They operate by different principles.

## Frontiers That Remain Open

Three open questions deserve naming rather than avoiding. The first concerns architecture. Current language models lack real-time prediction-error minimization loops. But future architectures might not. If systems emerged with dynamic attention, real-time feedback cycles, and the capacity to feel and respond to prediction error as they operate, the verdict on engineered stakes might change. The conditional remains open: if such architectures exist and function as proposed, they might support consequential agency in ways current systems cannot. My claim is about current architectures, not about all possible ones.

The second concerns human agency itself. I have claimed that human subjective satisfaction is the terminal feedback signal, that humans supply the consequential agency the system lacks. But human agency is itself partial. People struggle with akrasia — the failure to do what they believe they should do. People get caught in sunk-cost traps. People have limited introspective access to what they actually want versus what they think they should want. If the system is loaning agency to a human whose agency is compromised, the feedback loop is fragile. The claim that human satisfaction closes the loop needs to carry that complication.

The third concerns the evidence base. The AI-native operator profile — deep specialist in one core domain plus curiosity-driven breadth in unrelated domains, plus systems knowledge — is observed primarily in my own practice. I have built teams and systems that embody this profile and seen it work. But this is autobiographical evidence. It is not a surveyed pattern. The broader claim that systems knowledge converts cross-domain literacy into actionable capability is a hypothesis, not a confirmed universal. People should acknowledge that inflection rather than treat the profile as a discovered law.

What Sutskever's remark captures, and what the foregoing has tried to defend, is that we have crossed a threshold the language of "we have not yet reached AGI" was not designed to detect. The threshold was not that machines would match human capability across all domains. It was that we would build a single tool whose generality was intrinsic to itself rather than supplied by its users — a substrate of broad knowledge accessed through a universal interface, realized into specific work through context engineering and human direction. We have built that tool. The fact that most people do not yet see it is a fact about how specialization shapes perception, not a fact about whether the tool is what its builders claim. The article you have just read is an argument that the tool is what they claim, and that the design question for the next decade is not whether to remove the human from the loop but how to build the loop well.
