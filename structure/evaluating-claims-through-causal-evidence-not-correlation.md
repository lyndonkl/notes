# Evaluating claims through causal evidence, not correlation

These notes converge on a single epistemic standard: correlation, plausibility, and intuition are not substitutes for causal evidence. A theory that explains everything explains nothing; a direction in activation space that correlates with a concept is not proof it implements the concept; a belief that predicts the same experiences whether true or false is not a belief about reality. The cluster is cross-source — Yudkowsky/Arbital's Bayesian epistemology and the Representation Engineering paper's four-stage evaluation framework (Correlation → Manipulation → Termination → Recovery) are independently instantiating the same principle at very different scales.

## Core Notes

- [[the-strength-of-a-model-is-defined-by-what-it-prohibits-not-by-what-it-explains|The strength of a model is defined by what it prohibits, not by what it explains]] — the foundational claim: explanatory power without exclusion is not explanation
- [[a-belief-that-predicts-identical-experiences-whether-true-or-false-is-not-a-belief-about-reality|A belief that predicts identical experiences whether true or false is not a belief about reality]] — the same principle applied to belief-formation: if truth and falsehood predict the same observations, the belief is not anchored in reality
- [[testing-a-belief-against-reality-requires-operational-criteria-not-intuitive-plausibility|Testing a belief against reality requires operational criteria, not intuitive plausibility]] — the methodological corollary: intuition is not a test
- [[the-strength-of-evidence-depends-on-how-unlikely-it-would-be-to-observe-that-evidence-if-the-claim-were-false|The strength of evidence depends on how unlikely it would be to observe that evidence if the claim were false]] — quantifies what distinguishes weak from strong evidence: the likelihood ratio, not the surface match
- [[for-every-expectation-of-evidence-there-is-an-equal-and-opposite-expectation-of-counterevidence|For every expectation of evidence there is an equal and opposite expectation of counterevidence]] — the symmetric consequence: if an observation would confirm a claim, its absence must disconfirm it
- [[correlation-between-direction-and-concept-is-evidence-for-presence-not-function-only-causal-intervention-proves-implementation|Correlation between a direction and a concept is evidence for presence, not evidence for function—only causal intervention reveals whether a direction implements a concept]] — the RepE instantiation: an activation direction correlated with a concept is not proof the model uses it; only intervention can establish mechanism

## Borderline / Adjacent

- [[extraordinary-claims-require-extraordinary-evidence-because-extreme-prior-odds-demand-equally-extreme-likelihood-ratios|Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence because extreme prior odds demand equally extreme likelihood ratios]] — specifies how likelihood ratios must scale with prior odds; foundational to the evidence-strength note but focused on magnitude rather than causation
- [[absence-of-evidence-is-evidence-of-absence-whenever-the-observation-was-more-likely-under-the-hypothesis|Absence of evidence is evidence of absence whenever the observation was more likely under the hypothesis]] — the symmetric partner to the expectation/counterevidence note; lives one logical step away
- [[mechanistic-interpretability-and-representation-engineering-answer-different-questions-using-incompatible-units-of-analysis|Mechanistic interpretability and representation engineering answer different questions using incompatible units of analysis]] — discusses what each method can and cannot establish causally, but as a meta-comparison of disciplines rather than the underlying principle

## Open Questions

- What is the weakest form of causal evidence that still counts as causal? Does activation-patching count, or only ablation + recovery?
- Is there a principled difference between "prohibits" in the model-strength sense and "predicts differential experience" in the belief sense, or are they the same claim at different scales?
- When is it rational to hold a belief despite having no operational test — e.g., in mathematics, where beliefs are justified deductively rather than empirically?
