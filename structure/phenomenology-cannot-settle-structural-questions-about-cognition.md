# Phenomenology cannot settle structural questions about cognition

These notes converge on a single epistemological principle: the felt character of conscious experience (how thinking feels, how choice feels, how reflection feels, how a belief feels credible) cannot serve as evidence for the structural properties of cognition. What something feels like from the inside tells you about the system's introspective access, not about the underlying mechanism. The cluster's value comes from its cross-domain emergence — the same principle was independently derived in the rationality literature (inside-view bias, operational criteria for testing beliefs) and in consciousness philosophy (free-flowing thought, free will, metacognition).

## Core Notes

- [[the-phenomenology-of-free-flowing-thought-is-not-evidence-that-cognition-is-rule-free|The phenomenology of free-flowing thought is not evidence that cognition is rule-free]] — Thinking feels unconstrained because the rules of cognition are outside introspective access, not because cognition is structurally rule-free.
- [[the-felt-experience-of-free-will-is-not-evidence-that-choice-is-structurally-free-of-determination|The felt experience of free will is not evidence that choice is structurally free of determination]] — Choice feels free because upstream causes are introspectively opaque, not because choice is undetermined.
- [[the-felt-experience-of-metacognition-is-not-evidence-that-reflection-is-structurally-distinct-from-object-level-cognition|The felt experience of metacognition is not evidence that reflection is structurally distinct from object-level cognition]] — Reflection feels recursive because awareness can report on its own contents, not because there is a structurally separate meta-level observer.
- [[the-inside-view-feels-more-credible-than-the-outside-view-because-it-contains-specific-details-not-because-it-is-more-reliable|The inside view feels more credible than the outside view because it contains specific details, not because it is more reliable]] — Felt credibility tracks specificity (a phenomenological property), not reliability (an epistemic one).
- [[testing-a-belief-against-reality-requires-operational-criteria-not-intuitive-plausibility|Testing a belief against reality requires operational criteria, not intuitive plausibility]] — The methodological corollary: intuitive plausibility is not a test, because plausibility is a property of the map (phenomenology), not of the territory.

## Open Questions

- Is there *any* phenomenological report that would count as evidence about cognitive structure, or is the asymmetry total?
- Does the principle extend to consciousness itself? Is "I feel conscious" as epistemically hollow as "my thinking feels free"?
- What is the converse — can structural evidence ever settle phenomenological questions, or is the asymmetry symmetric?
- If introspective reports cannot establish structural facts about cognition, what *can* they establish about? Are they evidence about anything other than themselves?
