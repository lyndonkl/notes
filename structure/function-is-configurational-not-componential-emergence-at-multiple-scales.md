# Function is configurational, not componential: Emergence at multiple scales

A pattern that recurs across scales: what a component does depends on its collective context, not on intrinsic properties. A cell's function is determined by the bioelectric field of its collective, not by anything inside the cell. A consciousness's character is determined by the configuration of senses and social context, not by intrinsic conscious machinery. An LLM's claim to consciousness, on Friston's framework, depends on whether it does social inference, not on its substrate. Intelligence is determined by organizational structure, not by individual cognitive capacity. The cluster's value is in making the principle visible across domains so it can be applied as an analytic tool — when looking at any apparent property of a component, ask whether it is really a property of the configuration the component participates in.

- [[intelligence-scales-through-social-organization-not-individual-cognitive-upgrades|Intelligence scales through social organization, not individual cognitive upgrades]] — The cognitive instance: intelligence is a property of organizational configuration, not of individual cognitive capacity.

## Open Questions

- Is "configuration is constitutive" universally true, or are there cases where intrinsic component properties genuinely matter and cannot be reduced to configurational effects?
- What is the relationship between this principle and emergence more broadly — is "configurational function" a synonym for "emergent function," or a more specific subset?
- If function is configurational, what does that imply about identity and persistence? When a configuration changes, does the function persist, transform, or simply end?
- Does the principle predict that some artifacts (LLMs trained on human social discourse) inherit configurational properties from their training context, even when their substrate is unlike biological neural tissue? If so, the configurational view supports a stronger claim to LLM cognitive capacities than substrate-based views would allow.
