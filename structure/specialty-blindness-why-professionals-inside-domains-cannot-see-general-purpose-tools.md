# Specialty Blindness: Why Professionals Inside Domains Cannot See General-Purpose Tools

Evaluating a general tool from inside a single professional specialty is structurally unable to perceive what makes the tool general — the specialty's evaluative frame, success criteria, and tooling expectations all push toward "is this good enough at MY domain?" while hiding that the same tool spans every other domain too. This is not ignorance but a perceptual feature of being embedded in a domain-specific evaluation context, and it has a historical cause in the cognitive habit of specialization produced by industrial-era professional culture. The AI-native operator profile that escapes this blindness is a specific shape — deep specialist in one core domain plus curiosity-driven depth in a handful of unrelated side domains, plus systems knowledge to compose across them — made possible because language models collapse the implementation cost of acting on side-domain reading. Engineers are subject to the same blindness as everyone else; their version is more visible because they're closer to the tool.

## Open Questions

- Does specialty-bound underestimation correct itself when professionals see colleagues in other fields use the same tool, or does the specialty boundary remain cognitively binding even with external evidence?
- Is the AI-native operator profile causally productive (the deep + curiosity-broad shape itself produces value) or correlationally observed (the kind of person who acquires this shape also happens to build systems well)? The two have different implications for whether others should pursue the profile deliberately.
- The same structural argument should apply to AI researchers themselves — who are deepest in the specialty of building these tools. Does that community show similar blindness in different form?
